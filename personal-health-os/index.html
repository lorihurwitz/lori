Thesis II — Personal Health OS

The trust layer
that AI alone

Consumer healthcare is undergoing a structural shift from episodic to continuous. The constraint has never been technology — it has been trust. The platform that wins the front door to care won't be the fastest AI. It will be the one that earned the right to touch medical decisions.

The Core Thesis

From episodic to continuous — and trust is the constraint

The US healthcare system was built around scarcity. Physicians are scarce, appointments are scarce, and so care became episodic — something you access in discrete moments of acute need. Americans average 3–4 physician visits per year.1 Between those moments, the healthcare system largely disappears from your life.

AI removes the scarcity constraint. It can be available always, respond in seconds, personalize to your history, and scale to millions simultaneously. That structural shift — from episodic to continuous — is already underway. 3 in 5 US adults have used AI tools for healthcare questions in the past three months.2 The first stop in care is changing.

But AI creates a new constraint: trust at the clinical threshold. Searching Google for symptoms is low-stakes. Acting on a diagnosis, taking a medication, or deciding whether to go to the emergency room is not. The moment AI moves from information into guidance, the tolerance for error collapses. One bad interaction — one misdiagnosis, one flagged safety issue — destroys the relationship permanently. In healthcare, trust is not a UX problem. It is a regulatory, clinical, and psychological problem simultaneously.

"Unsupervised AI chatbots create safety and liability risks. Nurse lines drive over-escalation. Telehealth is slow and transactional. None of these is the right front door." — Counsel Health Series A announcement, October 2025

This is the investment thesis: the consumer healthcare infrastructure shift is real and large, but the durable winning position belongs to the platform that solves the trust problem structurally — not through better copy or brand safety theater, but through physician oversight baked into the architecture at every interaction. That is a fundamentally different product than what all the major players are building.

The underlying market forces reinforce the thesis from both the supply and demand side. Supply: physician shortages are structural and worsening — the AAMC now projects a shortage of up to 86,000 physicians by 2036, with primary care most affected. The system cannot staff its way out of this. Demand: patients have already shifted. They want faster, more accessible, always-on care — and are demonstrably willing to pay for it outside insurance. The $560B in out-of-pocket spending reflects not just necessity but revealed preference for speed and access over the traditional system.

The infrastructure transition is early innings. Consumer-facing digital health is one of the least-penetrated and fastest-growing layers of a $4.9T market. The question is not whether AI becomes the front door to care. It already is for hundreds of millions of queries weekly. The question is which architecture earns the right to capture that relationship permanently.

Competitive Landscape

The front door to care is already changing

People don't wait for a doctor's appointment to answer a health question anymore. They ask ChatGPT, search symptoms, message a friend in medicine. Roughly 1 in 3 adults now use AI for health advice before seeing a doctor, and 3 in 5 have used AI tools for health questions in the past three months. The question is not whether AI becomes the default first stop in care. It already is.

The more interesting question is which platforms earn the right to move from information to actual guidance — and do it in a way that democratizes access to care that has historically been gated by physician scarcity, cost, and geography. If you grew up with a doctor in your family, you had an unfair advantage: real-time, trusted, personalized guidance whenever something felt off. Everyone else waited 40 days for an appointment or Googled their symptoms. AI can collapse that gap permanently — but only if the platform on the other end has earned the right to be trusted with medical decisions.

"Growing up with a physician parent and being a physician myself, I know the magical feeling of having instant trusted advice anytime you need it about your health. How do we democratize that magical feeling to everyone?"

— Dr. Muthu Alagappan, CEO & Founder, Counsel Health

The incumbents are structurally misaligned with this moment. Below is a full accounting of who is competing for the front door — why each has a ceiling — and which three companies are building the right answer.

Three Expressions of the Same Shift

These companies are not competing with each other. They represent three different entry points into the same structural thesis: AI democratizing access to care that was previously gated by physician scarcity, cost, or geography — across acute care, mental health, and proactive health optimization.

Expression 01 — Acute & Continuous Care
Counsel Health
$36M raised a16z · GV · Pear VC Series A Licensed all 50 states Founded 2023
Target Investment

Counsel is the most direct expression of the democratization thesis: physician-supervised AI that makes real medical guidance available to anyone, instantly, without a 40-day wait. Every interaction is triaged by medical AI; licensed physicians step in for diagnosis, prescriptions, referrals, and follow-up until the issue is resolved — all asynchronously, through messaging, at a price point that works through payers and employers rather than requiring individuals to pay concierge rates.

The model is asynchronous by design, not by constraint. Inbox messaging rates in healthcare have nearly doubled annually for several consecutive years — people already prefer to communicate about health through text rather than scheduling a call. Counsel is building the clinical infrastructure for that modality to be taken seriously as a care model, not treated as a bolt-on feature. Their physician platform makes it economically viable to deliver supervised care at scale without the unit economics breaking.

On the enterprise side, Counsel replaces nurse lines as a direct line item in payer and employer benefits stacks. The replacement logic is simple: 96% issue resolution versus ~40–50% for nurse lines, physician response in 2 minutes versus 38-day appointment wait, and $381 saved per engaged member per year. The ROI argument is the close.

Expression 02 — Mental Health & Continuous Support
Jimini Health
$25M raised M13 · Town Hall · LionBird · Zetta Seed $17M raise March 2026 Founded 2023
Comparable

Mental health has the same access problem as primary care — compounded by stigma and an even more acute provider shortage. Jimini applies the same clinician-supervised AI architecture to behavioral health: an AI therapist assistant (Sage) handles between-session engagement while licensed therapists supervise every interaction. The gap between sessions is where most of daily mental health experience actually happens, and the traditional model leaves it entirely unsupported.

The go-to-market is B2B2C into behavioral health provider organizations rather than direct to consumers — a different distribution path than Counsel, but the same underlying thesis: AI extends the reach of scarce clinicians rather than replacing them, and trust comes from keeping a clinician in the loop. The $17M seed raise in March 2026 brings the total to $25M. The CEO previously co-founded Immunai, which reached a $1B+ valuation.

Why this matters for the thesis

Jimini validates that clinician-supervised AI is a category-level architecture, not a bet specific to Counsel. Two companies building the same structural answer in adjacent verticals — acute care and mental health — is a signal, not a coincidence. The CMS/FDA ACCESS and TEMPO programs in December 2025 created regulatory tailwinds for both simultaneously, which reinforces that the architecture is right, not just the individual companies.

Expression 03 — Proactive & Preventive Health
Superpower Health
$34M raised Forerunner Ventures Series A 150k-person waitlist (Apr 2025) Founded 2023
Adjacent

Superpower is the proactive expression of the thesis — democratizing access to the kind of comprehensive, data-driven health optimization that has historically been reserved for people wealthy enough to afford concierge medicine. Comprehensive biomarker panels, personalized longevity protocols, an AI health coach that compounds over time: these have always existed, but only at price points that excluded most people. Superpower brings the concierge model to a consumer price point.

It is not competing with Counsel for the same user moment. Counsel is where you go when something feels wrong. Superpower is where you go to stay ahead of something feeling wrong. Together they represent the full arc of continuous care — reactive and proactive, acute and preventive. Forerunner's backing is the right signal: this is a consumer identity and behavior-change play as much as a healthcare one, and Forerunner has a track record of identifying those shifts early.

Why this matters for the thesis

The 150k-person waitlist before a full consumer launch is a clear demand signal that consumers want proactive, data-rich health intelligence at a new price point. The behavioral shift toward proactive care is the longest-duration part of the thesis — harder to prove, but potentially the largest value pool. Superpower is the earliest and clearest bet on that leg of the shift.

Competitive Positioning

Where Counsel sits in the matrix

Two axes define the competitive landscape: the degree of clinical supervision (AI-only → physician-supervised) and the model of care (episodic/transactional → continuous/always-on). Counsel uniquely occupies the high-trust, continuous quadrant.

Investment Conviction

Why Counsel wins: the physician trust moat

Every major player in this landscape has made a deliberate architectural choice: keep humans out of the loop wherever possible. The cost economics are obvious — each physician interaction costs money; each AI response is nearly free. The entire category is being built on the assumption that AI quality will eventually earn consumer trust on its own.

Counsel made the opposite bet: physician oversight is not a cost to be engineered away. It is the product. That architectural choice creates a moat that pure-AI competitors cannot cross without rebuilding from scratch — because the moment they add physician supervision at scale, their unit economics break.

The Trust Moat: Physician Supervision at Every Interaction

Counsel's model integrates licensed physicians into every interaction at the point of clinical judgment — not as a backstop for escalations, but as a core product layer. Medical AI triages and provides context; physicians diagnose, prescribe, refer, and follow up. The AI doesn't replace physician judgment. It makes physician judgment scalable.

Why this moat is durable

Regulatory and liability position. Healthcare is uniquely regulated. An AI chatbot that provides a wrong answer about medication dosing faces existential regulatory and liability risk that a wrong restaurant recommendation does not. The CMS and FDA's December 2025 ACCESS and TEMPO programs sent an explicit signal: the government welcomes AI-enabled care models that keep clinicians in the loop. Unsupervised AI health platforms face increasing regulatory scrutiny; supervised ones face a clearing runway. Counsel's architecture is built to pass that bar structurally, not with compliance theater.

Enterprise buyer alignment. Counsel's go-to-market is payers and employers — the entities that are currently paying for nurse lines, telehealth benefits, and high-cost claims. These buyers don't just want a better consumer experience; they want clinical accountability and defensible ROI. Counsel's $381/member/year savings figure is the language these buyers speak. Pure AI platforms cannot make that same claim because the accountability chain requires a physician to close the loop. That's a structural sales advantage, not a marketing one.

The cold-start problem is solved. Physician trust is not built interaction by interaction — it is inherited from the physician credential. When a licensed physician is in the loop, the user doesn't need to evaluate whether the AI is reliable. The institutional trust of the medical profession transfers. This collapses the time-to-trust curve that every other consumer health AI platform is struggling to build organically.

Competitive impossibility. Amazon, Google, and OpenAI are structurally incentivized not to build this. Adding physician oversight at scale would require building a national clinical infrastructure (licensed in all 50 states), creating liability exposure they don't currently carry, and pricing the product in a way that is incompatible with their existing models. Teladoc built an episodic transaction model — retrofitting continuous physician supervision into that system would require rebuilding the product from scratch. Counsel has a head start that compounds with every member added.

The team signal

Counsel was founded by physicians and engineers. CEO Muthu Alagappan, MD, comes from clinical practice — which matters for a product where the core thesis is clinical trust. The a16z Bio + Health team (Julie Yoo, GP) led both the Seed and the Series A — an unusual double-down that signals conviction at the investor level that few early-stage companies earn. GV's General Partner Krishna Yeshwant, MD, MBA, joined the Series A — a physician-investor whose presence on the cap table is itself a trust signal for enterprise healthcare buyers.

Merits & Risks

Honest accounting

◆
AI is already the first stop in healthcare — and people know it

60% of US adults have used AI for healthcare questions in the past 3 months. This is not a behavior being created; it is a behavior being captured. The front door is already changing — the investment question is who captures the durable relationship, not whether the shift happens.2

◆
Out-of-pocket spend proves willingness to pay outside insurance

$560B in annual out-of-pocket healthcare spend growing at 6% YoY reflects revealed consumer preference for speed, access, and personalization. This enables business models that don't require navigating insurance complexity — a structural advantage for consumer-facing digital health at Counsel's price point.4

◆
Regulatory tailwinds are accelerating for supervised AI

CMS and FDA's December 2025 ACCESS and TEMPO programs created explicit incentives for clinician-in-the-loop AI in care delivery. Multiple commercial payers followed in February 2026. Counsel's architecture was built for this regulatory environment — not retrofitted to it.

◆
The physician shortage creates structural demand for this model

The AAMC projects a shortage of up to 86,000 physicians by 2036. 74 million Americans already live in Health Professional Shortage Areas. Counsel's model is not competing with physicians — it is the infrastructure that makes physicians scalable in a world where they are increasingly scarce.5

▲
Unit economics require physician efficiency at scale

The model works if AI can handle enough of the triage and context-gathering that physician time is deployed at the right decision points. The risk is that physician costs grow faster than revenue as the member base scales — particularly in high-acuity use cases that require more physician time per interaction. The 96% resolution rate is promising, but the economic question is how physician utilization per resolved interaction evolves as the platform moves beyond early adopters to general population members with more complex needs.

▲
Enterprise GTM means long sales cycles and procurement risk

Selling to payers and employers is a capital-intensive, long-cycle GTM motion. A single enterprise contract can take 12–18 months to close and requires navigating compliance, legal, and benefits procurement teams. The risk is runway consumption before sufficient enterprise traction materializes — particularly with well-funded incumbents offering alternative products in the same procurement conversation.

▲
Amazon is not standing still

Amazon Health AI launched in January 2026 with EHR data integration and clinical guardrails. While it is not physician-supervised in Counsel's model, Amazon has the distribution and data infrastructure to iterate rapidly. The risk is not that Amazon builds Counsel's exact model — it is that Amazon's AI health product becomes "good enough" for most consumer queries, narrowing the addressable market for physician-supervised alternatives to higher-acuity cases only.

▲
Behavioral shift to proactive care is unproven at scale

The long-term thesis on continuous care requires users to engage proactively, not just reactively. Americans average 3–4 doctor visits per year — a deeply ingrained episodic pattern. Changing that default behavior at scale is a product and go-to-market challenge that Counsel has not yet had to solve at the size needed for the full thesis to materialize. The early 100k+ member base is promising, but the behavioral change question remains the hardest variable.

Thesis Summary

Where value accrues in consumer health

The consumer healthcare transition from episodic to continuous is structural and durable. The platforms that capture the front door to care will compound patient relationships into data, trust, and eventually the ability to shape downstream healthcare utilization — one of the highest-value positions in a $4.9T market.

Within that shift, the winning architecture is not the fastest AI. It is the one that earns the right to handle the decisions that matter. Physician-supervised AI does not trade speed for trust — it compounds both. Counsel has demonstrated that the model works: 96% resolution, 2-minute physician response, $381 per-member savings. Those numbers make the enterprise sales motion self-funding in terms of the ROI argument. The go-to-market is straightforward. The regulatory environment is favorable and becoming more so. The incumbent products being replaced are structurally misaligned with what buyers need.

The trust moat in consumer healthcare is not a brand claim. It is an architectural decision. Counsel made that decision at founding. The rest of the market is catching up.

The investment conviction rests on a single observation: when something goes wrong in a medical interaction — and in a system handling millions of queries, something eventually will — the platform with a physician in the loop survives it. The platform without one may not. That asymmetry is priced into Counsel's architecture, and not into any of its competitors'.
